Working Papers

Currency Premium and Capital Accumulation with External Habit

Job Market Paper
Abstract: Diﬀerences in capital-output ratios across countries are large and persistent, even within developed countries. Empirically, capital-output ratios are negatively correlated with risk-free rates and currency returns, suggesting currency risk as a potential channel to explain the observed heterogeneity. I investigate the quantitative importance of this channel by building a multi-country general equilibrium model of capital accumulation with external habits. In my model, countries diﬀer in their loadings on a global shock. Currencies of high-loading countries appreciate when a negative global shock hits, making them a good hedge against global bad times. In equilibrium, such countries oﬀer lower currency returns, have permanently lower risk-free rates and accumulate more capital. My model features external habit to circumvent the well-known problem that standard CRRA preferences generate tiny risk premia. Setting the sensitivity parameters to be the same across countries, external habit generates signiﬁcant currency premia and diﬀerences in capital-output ratios. I estimate the model using GDP data from countries issuing the G10 currencies, and ﬁnd that the model generates 36% of the absolute capital-output ratio diﬀerence between Japan and New Zealand. The estimated loadings on the global shock explains 70% of the cross-country variations in capital-output ratios.

Work In Progress

A Currency Premium Puzzle

With Tarek A. Hassan and Thomas M. Mertens. Draft coming soon.
Abstract: Interest rate differences are large and persistent across countries and they account for most of the currency premia that carry traders earn. Expected change in exchange rate is close to 0, and investors expect high interest rate currency to depreciate. We show that these empirical regularities posit a challenge for canonical asset pricing models under complete market. Specifically, the facts require large differences in the variances of the log SDFs across countries (currency premium) without inducing counter-factual movements in the means of the log SDFs (change in exchange rates). However, to ensure risk-free rates are as stable as in the data when generating large risk-premia, the first and second moments of the log SDF are negatively correlated under canonical asset pricing models, resulting in high interest rate currencies appreciating and expected change in exchanges rate accounting for most of the currency premia. We analytically show that such a problem exists in both long-run risk and external habit models in general, and confirm our finding in a wide range of papers aiming at matching currency premia with these models.
